## Reference Model Analysis

Four models. Each teaches NC something specific.

---

### Rijksmuseum

**What it does:** Deep editorial, CC0 collection, Vermeer *Night Watch* signature object strategy,
high-quality print shop. 700,000+ objects online. Object-level provenance at every page.

**Strengths:**
- *Signature object strategy.* The Rijksmuseum built a brand around a single work
  (*De Nachtwacht*) — close-up detail campaigns, restoration live-streams, the object as
  editorial event. NC needs the same relationship to one illustration per collection.
- *CC0 commitment as brand identity.* Rijksmuseum's 2011 decision to release the collection
  CC0 was a public act. NGA and CMA/Met followed. NC's commercial model depends on that
  chain of decisions.
- *Print shop standard.* Rijksmuseum sells museum-quality prints at accessible price points
  with institutional provenance in the product description. This is the commerce model NC
  follows for NC-PROD-001 and NC-PROD-002.

**Gaps NC fills:**
- Rijksmuseum domain is Dutch Golden Age + European art. It has no Owen Jones, no David
  Roberts, no Wilson natural history, no Catherwood. Its geographic and illustration-type
  coverage is deliberately narrow. NC covers what Rijksmuseum cannot.
- Rijksmuseum's discovery is faceted browse (era, material, style, artist) — not graph
  traversal. You can find similar objects; you cannot follow an expedition.

**NC lesson:** Signature object per collection. One work per collection that carries the brand.
Object-level editorial depth before product page. Print shop quality standard.

---

### Smithsonian Institution

**What it does:** 19 museums + research centres + magazine. Natural history, American art,
air and space, world cultures. Magazine as editorial discovery engine. Strong expedition
and exploration framing across all collections.

**Strengths:**
- *Expedition narrative.* The Smithsonian knows how to frame a voyage — the Smithsonian
  Tropical Research Institute, the Ocean Hall, the Air & Space expeditions all carry
  the same "humans went somewhere and learned something" arc that NC's expedition chains use.
- *Narrative journalism standard.* Smithsonian Magazine writes "why this matters today"
  stories about objects and places. This is the editorial register NC needs for collection
  copy — not art history, not catalogue description, but consequence journalism.
- *Multi-institution coherence.* 19 separate museums feel like one institution because the
  editorial voice is unified. NC's 5 signature collections across Europeana, Walters, NHM,
  NGA, Met/CMA need the same unifying voice.

**Gaps NC fills:**
- Smithsonian has no commerce strategy for its illustrations. The Smithsonian Store sells
  merchandise (branded mugs, tote bags). The collections are not commercially activated.
- Smithsonian's discovery architecture is silo-by-museum, not cross-institutional graph.
  You cannot follow the Cook voyage from Smithsonian Air & Space through Smithsonian NMNH
  to Smithsonian Freer — those are three separate website experiences.
- Smithsonian Freer/Sackler is the most direct competitor to NC on Asian art. NC
  differentiates on ICH commerce path and structured discovery.

**NC lesson:** Expedition framing for South Georgia, Canary Islands, Chichén Itzá.
Consequence journalism register for collection copy. Unified editorial voice across
all 5 collections — they are not five separate things.

---

### National Geographic

**What it does:** Place-first. Photography as primary medium. "The best photographers in
the world, the best writers in the world." Yellow frame = instant quality signal. Adventure
+ wonder + responsibility (conservation since 1888).

**Strengths:**
- *Hero image primacy.* NatGeo's entire editorial model is organised around a single
  dominant photograph per story. The equivalent in NC is the signature image per collection.
  Owen Jones Court of Lions plate = NC's NatGeo cover. Wilson King Penguin = NC's
  NatGeo wildlife photograph.
- *Place-first discipline.* Every NatGeo story is anchored to a specific latitude and
  longitude. NC is the same — place is the organising principle, illustration is the
  evidence. This is structural alignment, not coincidence.
- *"The journey to the image."* NatGeo's expedition narrative is: we went there, we brought
  back proof. NC's narrative is structurally identical: they went there in 1839/1903/1842,
  they brought back proof. The proof is older. The proof is the whole point.

**Gaps NC fills:**
- NatGeo sells the photograph *after* the camera arrived. NC has the record *before* the
  camera arrived. For every place NC covers, there is a pre-photographic visual record that
  NatGeo cannot access because photography didn't exist yet. This is NC's structural
  competitive advantage over NatGeo, and it is permanent.
- NatGeo has no discovery graph. You can read a NatGeo story about Petra, and then another
  about Alhambra, but no NatGeo product tells you that David Roberts drew both in the same
  seven-year journey. NC does.
- NatGeo has no commerce strategy for the primary visual record. NatGeo licenses
  photographs; it does not sell the expedition illustration.

**NC lesson:** Hero image discipline — one signature image per collection that carries the
same immediate visual authority as a NatGeo cover photograph. "Before the camera" is the
editorial frame NC uses wherever it competes with photographic content.

---

### Google Arts & Culture

**What it does:** 2,000+ institutional partners, millions of objects. Art Camera (gigapixel
zoom). Color explorer, virtual tours, art selfie. Discovery by serendipity. Zero commerce.

**Strengths:**
- *Technical access.* Google A&C has made previously inaccessible PD content browsable.
  Europeana, NGA, CMA, Met, Walters are all on Google A&C in high resolution.
- *Scale.* No other platform approaches Google A&C's breadth. This is simultaneously its
  advantage and its weakness.
- *Surprise mechanism.* "Explore today" and color-based discovery introduce users to works
  they would never have searched for. This is serendipitous discovery without structure.

**Gaps NC fills:**
- *Zero commerce.* Google A&C does not sell anything. NC is the commercial layer that
  Google A&C explicitly refuses to build. Every Owen Jones plate, every David Roberts
  lithograph, every Wilson watercolour accessible on Google A&C is available to buy
  exclusively on NC.
- *Zero structured discovery.* Google A&C tells you that two works are "in the same style"
  or "from the same era." NC tells you that Owen Jones drew the Alhambra, then used the
  same plates to write *The Grammar of Ornament*, which shaped the Great Exhibition, which
  led to the V&A, which has Owen Jones plates in its founding collection. That is a
  discovery journey. Google A&C offers proximity; NC offers consequence.
- *Zero editorial identity per object.* Google A&C shows object metadata. NC tells the
  story of why the object matters, who made it, where they went, what happened next.

**The critical implication:** The same PD works that appear on Google A&C without editorial
context or commerce are NC's entire product inventory. Google A&C is the proof that these
works are accessible. NC is the proof that accessible is not the same as activated.

**NC lesson:** Structured discovery is the differentiator — not broader, not higher resolution,
but *more meaningful*. NC is Google A&C plus the graph plus the story plus the product.

## The 5 Questions

### 1. Which collection should launch first?

**Answer: Alhambra (NC-COL-001)**

The launch-first collection must clear four tests simultaneously:

**Test 1: Does the hero image require explanation to sell?**
- Alhambra (Owen Jones pattern): No. A geometric pattern communicates visually before language.
- Petra (Roberts *Khazneh*): Moderate. The image is beautiful but the story amplifies it.
- South Georgia (Wilson penguin): No — but "king penguin watercolour" requires more context than "Moorish tile pattern."
- Hudson River (Cole *Voyage of Life*): Yes — the allegory requires the title to land.
- Kyoto (Rakuchū-rakugai-zu): Yes — "court screen painting" requires cultural context.

Owen Jones patterns are the only hero image in the 5 that communicate their own commercial value without editorial support. This matters at launch when no editorial infrastructure is yet proven.

**Test 2: Does the collection differentiate NC from all 4 reference models simultaneously?**
The Alhambra scores ✦ against all 4 reference models. No other collection does this. Every competitor either has no Alhambra content (Rijksmuseum, NatGeo on the illustration side), has the content without commerce or editorial (Google A&C), or has adjacent but different content (Smithsonian Freer). The Alhambra is NC's most exclusive territory.

**Test 3: Does the hero product establish NC's brand positioning?**
Owen Jones patterns establish that NC sells *applied* PD illustration — the design that came from the object, not a photograph of the object. This is NC's structural differentiation from NatGeo (they photograph places; NC illustrates them) and from Google A&C (they catalogue files; NC activates them). Launch with patterns and you define what NC is. Launch with a landscape painting and you look like a fine art print shop.

**Test 4: Is the story measurably consequential?**
Owen Jones → Great Exhibition → V&A founding methodology → Victorian industrial design. The consequence is traceable and verifiable. Every subsequent collection can meet this standard because the Alhambra demonstrated it first.

**Verdict:** The Alhambra launches first because it is the one collection where the product explains itself, the territory is uncontested, the brand position is set, and the consequence is provable.

### 2. Which collection has highest revenue potential?

**Answer: Alhambra (NC-COL-001)**

Revenue = volume × price × format applicability × repeat purchase rate.

| Metric | Alhambra | Petra | South Georgia | Hudson River | Kyoto |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Volume** | Design + art + homeware market | Art print market | Premium nature illustration market | US domestic art print market | Japanese art + craft market (after SA) |
| **Price ceiling** | Mid (pattern) + high (framed giclée) | High (single signature work) | High (premium provenance) | Mid-to-high | Mid now; high after SA |
| **Format applicability** | Print · Tile · Textile · Stationery · Wallpaper · Digital | Print · Framed · Collector's | Print · Framed · Collector's | Print · Framed · Digital | Print · Framed · Textile (after SA) |
| **Repeat purchase rate** | High (pattern series: 139 remaining Alhambra plates beyond launch selection) | Moderate (5-6 Petra subjects, less repeat) | Moderate (Wilson series: 2 hero works + supporting) | High (NGA has hundreds of deployable works) | High (after SA: TC products are a catalogue) |
| **Rights cost** | Zero (Europeana PD) | Zero (Walters/Europeana PD) | Zero (NHM CC0) | Zero (NGA CC0) | Zero (CMA/Met CC0) |

**Alhambra wins on format applicability.** A landscape painting sells as a print. A geometric pattern sells as a print, a tile, a textile, a wallpaper, a phone case, a stationery set, a tote bag. The market for the Alhambra's illustration type is structurally larger than the market for any other type in the 5.

Owen Jones's *Grammar of Ornament* alone has 112 plates, of which 37 are derived from the Alhambra. That is 37 deployable hero images from one work. Add *Plans of the Alhambra* (102 plates) and the total addressable catalogue from this single collection is 139 distinct illustrations before NC activates any secondary sources (Roberts, Doré).

**Kyoto is the long-term revenue leader, not the short-term one.** After SA-ICH-PLACE-001, Nishijin-ori TC textile design products enter the highest-margin category in the NC portfolio. At that point, Kyoto may overtake Alhambra on revenue per SKU. But until SA ratification, Alhambra is the clear leader on volume × format applicability.

**Hudson River is the runner-up.** NGA CC0 covers hundreds of works. The American landscape print market is the largest domestic US segment. But landscape prints are a single format; geometric patterns are many formats. Alhambra volume × format × repeat purchase outperforms Hudson River volume × single format.

### 3. Which collection best demonstrates the discovery graph?

**Answer: Hudson River (NC-COL-007)**

The discovery graph demonstration requires that a user can *traverse it* — click from one place to another and feel that the connection was earned, not random. At launch, that requires live places on both ends of the edge.

**Edges live on day one, by collection:**

| Collection | Live edges | Traversal |
|---|---|---|
| Alhambra | Alhambra only (Petra, Fez are in activation queue, not yet live) | 0 traversable edges at day 1 |
| Petra | Petra only (same problem) | 0 traversable edges at day 1 |
| South Georgia | SC-PLACES-001 pilot: `antarctic-peninsula` (penguin taxon trail) | 1 traversable edge |
| **Hudson River** | `yellowstone` · `yosemite` · `grand-canyon` · `olympic` — all NC-PLACES-001 pilot places | **4 traversable edges from day 1** |
| Kyoto | `mount-fuji` (NC-PLACES-001) via CMA/Met Japanese art | 1 traversable edge |

Hudson River is the only collection whose discovery graph works on day one without waiting for any other activation. A user at Hudson River clicks "Yellowstone" and arrives at a place page that already exists in the NC portfolio. They can see Cole → Moran → Yellowstone as a chain. That is the discovery graph, demonstrating itself, with live data.

The mechanism: Thomas Cole (1801–1848) painted the Hudson Valley. Frederic Church (1826–1900) extended Cole's visual language to Niagara and South America. Thomas Moran (1837–1926) carried that visual language into Yellowstone and the Grand Canyon — he was the artist whose paintings persuaded Congress to make Yellowstone a national park. That lineage is a discovery traversal. All four destination places (Yellowstone, Yosemite, Grand Canyon, Olympic) are in the NC pilot portfolio. The graph is not theoretical. It is live.

**This is the discovery graph demonstration vehicle precisely because it proves the graph
works with existing content.** The other collections build the graph forward. Hudson River
proves it backward — it connects to what is already there.

**What the visitor experiences:** Arrive at Hudson River. Click "Expedition Trail (USGS surveys)." See Yellowstone, Yosemite, Grand Canyon, Olympic. Click Yellowstone. See the Moran painting. See the connection backward to Cole. See the NC pilot content (existing Moran/landscape context). Return to Hudson River and follow a different axis (Illustrator's World) to find the Durand → Cole lineage. This is a working discovery journey, not a diagram.

### 4. Which collection best demonstrates ICH?

**Answer: Kyoto (NC-COL-006)**

ICH demonstration requires three things: registered elements, a commerce path, and a place page that explains the difference between documenting a living practice and commodifying it.

| Collection | ICH elements | Domain | Commerce path |
|---|---|---|---|
| Kyoto | **5** (Nishijin-ori + Noh + Gion Matsuri + Yamahoko + Kumiodori) | **TC + PA** | TC: Nishijin-ori design prints (after SA-ICH-PLACE-001) |
| Alhambra | 1 (Flamenco) | PA | Contextual only (PA domain, no direct commerce) |
| Petra | 0 | — | — |
| South Georgia | 0 | — | — |
| Hudson River | 0 | — | — |

Kyoto is the only collection in the 5 with a TC domain ICH element — the only domain where a direct commerce path exists. Flamenco (Alhambra) is PA domain: it can provide editorial context but cannot be the product. Nishijin-ori silk weaving is TC domain: the design documentation of the weaving pattern is the product, and it is fully separable from the practice itself.

**The NC ICH thesis, demonstrated by Kyoto:**
The ICH element is not the product. The PD illustration of the practice, made before living memory, is the product. Nishijin-ori silk weaving has been documented in manuscript pattern books since the 8th century. Those pattern books, produced before any current weaver was born, are the commercial object. NC sells the historical record of the pattern; the weavers in Nishijin keep the practice. There is no extraction, no appropriation, no commodification of living craft — there is a commercial illustration archive and a living tradition, operating independently on the same subject.

This distinction is the entire NC ICH governance position (ICH-1: practice ≠ product). Kyoto is the place where that distinction is most visibly demonstrated, because Nishijin-ori is still in production, in the same district, in the same city, using patterns continuous with those in the Met and CMA collections. The connection between the historical PD record and the living practice is unambiguous — and the boundary between them is clear.

**After SA-ICH-PLACE-001:** The Nishijin-ori TC design print activates. Kyoto becomes the proof of concept for the entire NC ICH commerce model. If it works for Nishijin-ori, the same model applies to Silk Road ikat (Samarkand), Persian carpet motifs (Isfahan), and Arabic calligraphy (Fez). Kyoto is not just the best ICH demonstration. It is the template for all subsequent ICH commerce.

### 5. Which collection best demonstrates public-domain value?

**Answer: South Georgia (NC-COL-002)**

Public-domain value has two dimensions: the philosophical argument (why PD matters) and the commercial argument (what PD makes possible). The best demonstration covers both.

**The philosophical argument:**

Wilson painted these penguins in 1903 aboard a scientific expedition. He was not a commercial artist. He was a physician and naturalist who made watercolours because it was the only way to record what he saw accurately. He died in 1912 on Scott's return from the South Pole — not from cold, but from the exhaustion of keeping morale among men who knew they were going to die. The Natural History Museum held his paintings for a century. In 2014, they released them CC0.

No rights negotiation. No licensing fee. No usage restriction. The work of a man who gave his life to produce scientific knowledge became freely available to every person on earth — not because copyright expired automatically, but because an institution made a deliberate decision to give it away.

That decision is the entire NC business model compressed into one act.

**The commercial argument:**

Wilson's watercolours are not accessible through photography, Google Image Search, or any other route that bypasses the NHM's digital archive. They are not on Google A&C. They are not in any stock photo library. The only place you can buy a print of Wilson's king penguin, made at the standard NC is building toward, is from an institution that has decided to activate the NHM's CC0 commitment as a commerce product. That institution is NC.

**Why not Alhambra for PD demonstration?**

Owen Jones's commercial deployment is so continuous and so extensive that it risks making PD look *inevitable* — of course those patterns are freely used, they've been used for 170 years. The Wilson story makes PD feel like a *decision* — someone chose to keep this, someone chose to release it, someone chose to build a business on it. That chain of choices is the PD argument.

**The demonstration moment:**

A visitor who understands that Wilson died on the ice in 1912, that NHM held his paintings, that NHM chose to release them CC0, and that they are now available as a precision giclée printed at NC's quality standard — that visitor understands why public domain is not an abstract legal category. It is the mechanism by which a dead naturalist's visual science becomes available to every collector in the world. The PD is the bridge between Wilson's 1903 expedition and 2026 commerce. South Georgia is where that bridge is most visible.
