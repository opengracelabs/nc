## Canon Statement

The Nature & Culture Canon is the definitive list of 100 illustration opportunities that together constitute NC's institutional identity. Where NC-MASTERPIECES-001 ranks works commercially, the Canon ranks them editorially. A work earns Canon status by being irreplaceable: by documenting something that cannot be seen any other way — a lost species, a discovered continent, a plant named for the first time, a landscape painted on the day it was surveyed.

The Canon is organized into eight series. Each series is a chapter in a single continuous story: humanity watching, recording, and understanding the natural world over three centuries. The Canon begins with extinction — the losses that give everything else its urgency — and ends with the view from orbit: the moment we finally saw the whole.

**Canon works carry five obligations beyond those of NC Masterpieces:**
1. Every Canon work eventually has a full NC-7-link provenance chain, published on its place page.
2. Every Canon work earns a named editorial story — not a product description but a narrative.
3. Every Canon series earns a permanent editorial page on the NC website.
4. Canon works above 54/60 are CE-eligible regardless of activation wave.
5. The Canon is reviewed annually. Works may be added; none may be removed.

## Scoring

Canon entries are scored on the NC-MASTERPIECES-001 six-criterion framework: CS (Cultural Significance) + SS (Scientific Significance) + PD (Public-Domain Confidence) + CV (Collection Value) + CoV (Commerce Value) + EV (Educational Value), each 0–10, max 60. Works already registered in NC-MASTERPIECES-001 carry their registry scores. New Canon entries carry Canon-assigned scores, pending NC-MASTERPIECES-001 registration.

**Canon threshold:** ≥ 47/60. Works below threshold may be included on editorial grounds; a Canon Note is required explaining the exception.

The Archive of Loss does not trade in nostalgia. It trades in evidence.

### Anchor Works — Extinction

**NC-CA-EX-001 Thylacine.** Benjamin, the last known Thylacine, died in Hobart Zoo on 7 September 1936. Gould's illustration preceded the extinction by 91 years. The NHM holds the definitive natural history plate — the only quality image of a living Thylacine family before the species collapsed. This is the highest-scoring single work in the Canon, and the most important institutional acquisition in DD-NHM-001.

**NC-CA-EX-002 / EX-003 Carolina Parakeet + Passenger Pigeon.** These two are always sold as a pair. North America's only endemic parrot and the world's most numerous bird — both extinguished within six years of each other (1914, 1918). Audubon painted both. The paired portfolio is NC's first Extinction Archive commercial release, and the most powerful conservation narrative in our commerce programme.

**NC-CA-EX-009 Dodo.** George Edwards' 1758 illustration in "Gleanings of Natural History" is the most scientifically rigorous pre-extinction depiction of the Dodo and is available from BHL at Wave 1. Savery's 1626 painting (Ashmolean) is the more famous cultural image; however, Savery never saw a live Dodo and may have worked from a stuffed specimen. Edwards worked from written accounts and specimen data. Both are cited in the Canon record. The BHL Edwards version activates now.

### Anchor Works — Exploration

**NC-CA-EXP-001 Darwin's Finches.** This is the illustration of evolution. John Gould reclassified Darwin's field specimens into four distinct species during the 1837–39 publication of the Beagle zoology. The four finch plates, placed together, are the visual evidence Darwin used to reconstruct the mechanism of natural selection. NC-PROD-005 is active. No single work in the Canon better demonstrates the commercial power of scientific illustration.

**NC-CA-EXP-007 Emperor Penguin with Chick.** Edward Wilson painted this during the Discovery Expedition (1901–04), in Antarctica, before Scott's fatal South Pole journey in which Wilson himself died. The SPRI holds the originals. DD-SPRI-001 is NC's most important blocked institutional document: without it, the South Georgia Signature Collection (NC-COL-002) has no anchor.

**NC-CA-EXP-012 King Penguin, Forster.** Johann Reinhold Forster's son Georg painted the first European illustration of a King Penguin during Cook's Second Voyage (1772–75). This is distinct from Wilson's Emperor Penguin at EXP-007. The Forster is available from BHL at Wave 1 and serves as a bridge for the South Georgia collection until SPRI is resolved.

### Anchor Works — Botany

**NC-CA-BOT-001 Rosa centifolia.** Pierre-Joseph Redouté was called "the Raphael of flowers" by his contemporaries. His Rosa centifolia — the Cabbage Rose, grown in the gardens of Malmaison for the Empress Joséphine — is the work that made him. Botanical illustration has produced hundreds of thousands of plates. This one is the standard by which all others are measured. BHL T1 activates now; MNHN high-resolution follows DD-MNHN-001.

**NC-CA-BOT-012 Dictyota dichotoma, Atkins.** Anna Atkins published "Photographs of British Algae: Cyanotype Impressions" in 1843 — the first book illustrated with photographs. She placed algae specimens directly on photosensitive paper and exposed them to sunlight, creating contact prints of extraordinary precision and beauty. This is not illustration; it is the organism itself, rendered in cyanotype blue. Its Canon inclusion marks the boundary between the illustration tradition and the photographic tradition that succeeded it.

### Anchor Works — Birds

**NC-CA-BRD-001 Wild Turkey, Audubon.** Plate I of The Birds of America was Audubon's declaration of intent. He painted the Wild Turkey — Benjamin Franklin's preferred national symbol — at life-size, on double elephant folio, before any American printer had attempted anything comparable. It is the opening page of the greatest natural history publication in American history. The BHL version activates at Wave 1. The Smithsonian elephant folio version is the highest-priority output of DD-SMITHSONIAN-001.

**NC-CA-BRD-008–010 The Lear Macaws and Cockatoo.** Edward Lear published his "Illustrations of the Family of Psittacidae" at the age of nineteen, before he became famous as a poet. These three plates — Hyacinth Macaw, Scarlet Macaw, Sulphur-crested Cockatoo — represent the peak of Victorian ornithological illustration: vivid, accurate, and commercially versatile across every NC product family. They remain among the most reproduced natural history plates in the world.

**NC-CA-BRD-015 Peregrine Falcon, Joseph Wolf.** Joseph Wolf is NC's eighth Priority Illustrator, the least famous and the most underutilized. His peregrine falcon from Gould's "Birds of Great Britain" (1862–73) is the definitive portrait of the animal — not a specimen study but a living predator caught in attitude. Wolf understood animal behavior in a way that no illustrator before him had attempted. This plate is the canonical argument for Wolf's inclusion in NC's Priority Illustrator list.

### Anchor Works — Marine Life

**NC-CA-MAR-001 Radiolaria, Haeckel.** This is NC's most commercially deployable Wave 1 work after Earthrise. The Radiolaria plate is the founding document of Art Nouveau's organic geometry — its influence runs directly through Guimard's Paris Métro entrance, Sullivan's ornamental programme, and a century of decorative arts. It is also rigorous 19th-century protist science. No blocking action. No institution DD required. It activates the moment NC decides it should. The Radiolaria plate is the argument for why public domain illustration is a commercial asset.

**NC-CA-MAR-011 Blue Morpho, Merian.** The Blue Morpho sequence is Merian's most visually spectacular work: the complete metamorphosis of a South American morpho butterfly, shown with the plant it depends on, rendered in colour at a time when colour natural history illustration barely existed. It fits the Marine Life series because Merian was the first scientist to document life cycles — the biology of dependence, not just the biology of appearance. It is Cross-series with BOTANY and EXTINCTION (the fate of Suriname's insects today).

## VI. DEEP TIME

*Before Memory.*

These works document the Earth before humans. They are illustrations of deep time: the Ichthyosaurus pulled from Jurassic shale at Lyme Regis, the pterodactyl reconstructed by Cuvier from a limestone slab, the geological map that proved England sat on layered strata like pages of a book. The Deep Time series is NC's most intellectually ambitious. It asks the question that every natural history museum tries to answer: how do you make 500 million years feel real?

### Anchor Works — Deep Time

**NC-CA-DT-002 Duria Antiquior.** Henry De la Beche commissioned William Conybeare to sketch a scene of Jurassic Dorset for Mary Anning — the woman who had found most of the fossils in it. The resulting lithograph (1830) is the first reconstruction of a prehistoric world as a *scene* rather than a specimen catalogue. It invented the genre of palaeoart. The ichthyosaurs and plesiosaurs it depicts were found by Anning on the beach at Lyme Regis. She never received credit. NC's editorial note for this work must restore it.

**NC-CA-DT-010 William Smith Geological Map.** Smith's 1815 map of England and Wales is the work that made geology a science. By following the same fossil assemblages across hundreds of miles of country, Smith proved that rock strata were laid down in a predictable, dated sequence. He called it "the map that changed the world." It is available from BHL, it is pre-1928, and it connects directly to Lyme Regis, Derbyshire, and the entire English geological landscape that NC's Deep Time series documents.

### Anchor Works — Culture

**NC-CA-CUL-001 Court of Lions, Owen Jones.** Owen Jones spent two winters at the Alhambra in 1833–34 and 1837, measuring and recording every surface. His resulting "Plans and Details of the Alhambra" (1842–45) was the publication that taught Victorian England what Islamic ornament was and could be. The Moresque patterns he documented became the vocabulary of a generation of designers. 170 years later, they are still in commercial production. NC-COL-001 (Alhambra) is the highest-scoring Signature Collection; this plate is its anchor.

**NC-CA-CUL-006 The Great Wave, Hokusai.** Under the Wave off Kanagawa is the most reproduced artwork in history. Its wave curve was Hokusai's 40-year study of water in motion. It places Fuji — infinitely distant and infinitely stable — against a sea that is about to destroy everything in its path. The Canon includes it not because it needs an argument for inclusion, but because any Canon that excluded it would be asking why.

**NC-CA-CUL-009 El Castillo, Catherwood.** Frederick Catherwood was trained as an architectural draughtsman and accompanied John Lloyd Stephens on two expeditions to Central America (1839–40, 1841–42). His lithographs from "Views of Ancient Monuments in Central America, Chiapas and Yucatan" (1844) were the first accurate European documentation of Maya architecture. The Mayan world had been imagined, speculated about, and romanticized for 300 years. Catherwood drew what was actually there.

### Anchor Works — Planetary Perspective

**NC-CA-PLN-001 Earthrise.** This needs no argument. William Anders took AS08-14-2383 on Christmas Eve 1968 from lunar orbit during Apollo 8. It was the first time humans had seen the whole Earth — small, finite, completely alone. The photograph is credited by environmental historians as one of the primary catalysts for Earth Day, the Clean Air Act, and the modern environmental movement. It is a U.S. Government work under 17 U.S.C. § 105. It is NC-PROD-001. It is active. It is the Canon's last entry and its first commercial fact.

**NC-CA-PLN-007 Maury's Wind and Current Chart.** Matthew Fontaine Maury collected thousands of ships' logs from the US Navy archives and extracted the prevailing winds and currents of the Atlantic from their accumulated observations. His 1847 chart reduced sailing time from New York to California by 47 days and established physical oceanography as a discipline. It is the oldest piece of data science in this Canon, and BHL holds it. Its commercial arc for NC connects to every place touched by an Atlantic-crossing expedition.

**NC-CA-PLN-009 Tableau physique des Andes, Humboldt.** Alexander von Humboldt spent 1799–1804 in the Americas. His cross-section of Mount Chimborazo — showing species distributions by altitude, from the páramo to the snowline — was the first illustration to treat the Earth as a system: climate zones as nested layers of life, not isolated specimens. Every concept in modern ecology traces through this plate. The BHL version activates at Wave 1. MNHN provides the high-resolution upgrade when DD-MNHN-001 ratifies.
