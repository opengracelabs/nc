---

## Purpose

This document evaluates Nature & Culture not as a product, but as an institution. The
question is not "does the site look good?" but "would a person with authority trust this
platform with their scholarship, their money, their curriculum, or their reputation?"

The four reference institutions — Smithsonian, Rijksmuseum, National Geographic, Google
Arts & Culture — were selected because each represents a distinct trust model: scholarly
authority, collection authority, editorial authority, and access authority. NC aspires to
elements of all four. This audit documents what remains between aspiration and credential.

The findings are unflattering in places. That is the point. An institution that cannot
survive a rigorous external review has not yet earned the designation.

| Trust dimension | Current state | Gap severity |

## Four-Institution Lens

---

### Smithsonian Institution

**What the Smithsonian is:** 19 museums. 175 years. A Congressional charter. Curators with
named credentials and public research outputs. A peer-reviewed journal. Annual reports with
financial accountability. Named secretaries who are publicly answerable for collection
decisions. The Smithsonian Brand is, at its core, the credential of its named humans.

**What a Smithsonian curator would say reviewing NC:**

*"I can see that this institution has done serious governance work — the rights
frameworks referenced in the metadata appear more rigorous than anything most digital
archives produce. But I cannot see any of it. I cannot identify who curated this
collection. I cannot find a selection methodology. I cannot verify the authority
resolution standards cited. The governance documents referenced in footnotes are not
publicly available. Before I recommend this to a colleague, I need to know: who
decided that Owen Jones belongs here and David Roberts belongs here, and what process
led to those decisions? An institution is not its content. It is its process, made
visible."*

**What NC lacks in the Smithsonian frame:**
- Named staff with visible credentials (no curator names, no director, no research staff)
- Publicly accessible governance standards (all authority standards are internal)
- Peer-visible selection methodology (why these illustrations, not others?)
- A scholarly research output (no publications, no cited methodology)
- Institutional history (when was NC founded? By whom? With what mission?)
- Public accountability layer (no annual report, no board, no named decision-maker)

**The single-line diagnosis:** NC has Smithsonian-quality internal governance with no
Smithsonian-quality external accountability. The institution exists in private.

---

### Rijksmuseum

**What the Rijksmuseum is:** 220+ years as the Dutch national museum. Conservation labs
visible to the public. Rijksmuseum Research Library. Rijksmuseum Bulletin (peer-reviewed).
Named conservators. Named curators per collection. The 2011 CC0 release was a public,
cited decision made by a named director (Wim Pijbes) on a stated principle. The print shop
is not a side product — it is an institutional statement that the collection belongs to
everyone. Every print sold from the Rijksmuseum comes with a certificate that names the
work, the creator, the accession number, the paper specification, and the edition.

**What the Rijksmuseum print shop director would say reviewing NC:**

*"The rights position appears sound — public domain, clearly. But I am looking at a
product page and I cannot see: what paper this is printed on, what the archival rating
is, how the edition is controlled, or who produced it. When I sell a Vermeer print,
I give the collector a document they can keep for a century. It has eight fields.
They know the title, the creator, the museum accession number, the print edition
number, the paper weight, the archival rating, the print studio, and our conservation
recommendation for storage. This product has none of that. A collector who pays €300
for a print and receives it in a tube with no documentation has no way to prove what
they bought. That is not a print shop. That is a poster service."*

**What NC lacks in the Rijksmuseum frame:**
- Certificate of authenticity with defined fields
- Print specification disclosure (paper, print process, archival rating, colour profile)
- Edition control system (limited edition = a number, a registry, a process)
- Conservation guidance for the buyer
- The institutional accession relationship (the Rijksmuseum product links to the museum
  record; NC's product links to... nothing external and specific)
- A print studio identity (who physically makes these? Where?)

**The single-line diagnosis:** NC sells institutional-quality content with consumer-grade
product documentation. The product experience does not match the content quality.

### National Geographic

**What National Geographic is:** 136 years. A named Society behind the magazine. Bylined
field journalism — every story has an author, a photographer, an expert. Fact-checking
that is a brand attribute, not a back-office function. The yellow frame as a credential
that a specific quality process was applied. Named Explorers with public profiles.
Educational programs with formal school licensing and teacher resources. NatGeo's trust
comes from the byline.

**What a NatGeo editor would say reviewing NC:**

*"The copy is good. The register is right — place-first, consequence-focused, the 'why
this matters' structure is there. But I cannot find a byline anywhere on this platform.
I do not know who wrote the Alhambra collection copy. I do not know who selected the
Owen Jones plates. I do not know if the claim that Roberts drew the Khazneh in 1839
was verified, or just written. National Geographic has a fact-checking process. It
has named editors. It has corrections policies. When we make a claim about a place or
a work, someone is accountable for that claim. Here, I cannot find anyone. Strong
editorial copy written by an anonymous institution is indistinguishable from marketing
copy written by an anonymous copywriter. The difference is the byline."*

**What NC lacks in the NatGeo frame:**
- Bylines on collection copy (no named author on any editorial content)
- Named editorial voice (no editor-in-chief, no editorial board, no visible editorial
  process)
- A corrections/update policy (what happens when NC gets something wrong?)
- Verifiable claims (citations visible to the public, not just internal authority docs)
- Named experts consulted (which ornithologist confirmed the Wilson species identifications?
  Which historian confirmed the Roberts expedition dates?)
- Editorial standards published and publicly accessible
- No educational licensing program

**The single-line diagnosis:** NC has the editorial voice of National Geographic and the
editorial accountability of a brand content team. The copy trusts the reader; the
institution does not yet trust the public with its process.

---

### Google Arts & Culture

**What Google Arts & Culture is:** 2,000+ institutional partners. Partner logos on every
object page. Art Camera (gigapixel zoom as a visible technology investment). Street View
cultural integrations. A classroom mode for teachers. A free-to-access model that makes
the trust cost low. Google A&C's trust comes from institutional proxies — if the V&A
is a partner, the V&A's trust transfers to the platform. The viewer sees the museum logo
and the museum's credibility flows through.

**What the Google A&C product manager would say reviewing NC:**

*"This platform uses content from the Natural History Museum, the National Gallery of
Art, the Cleveland Museum of Art, the Walters Art Museum, and Europeana. These are
serious institutions. But I cannot see them on this platform. I see 'Public Domain'
in small text at the bottom of a product page. I do not see 'From the collection of
the Natural History Museum, London.' I do not see the NHM logo. I do not see a link
to the original NHM record. On Google Arts & Culture, the institutional partner is
the first visible element of every object page. The trust flows from them to us.
This platform has the same institutional relationships we do — but is hiding them.
That is a structural trust error. You are choosing to appear to stand alone when you
could be standing in front of five world-class institutions."*

**What NC lacks in the Google A&C frame:**
- Visible institutional partner acknowledgment at product level (NHM logo on Wilson print;
  NGA logo on Cole painting; Europeana/Walters logo on Roberts)
- Link to the source institutional record from every product page
- A free educational access tier (Google A&C has explicit free use for education)
- Accessibility compliance publication (WCAG audit not performed or published)
- No "discover more from this institution" pathway
- No visible partnership announcements or institutional relationships

**The single-line diagnosis:** NC has the institutional relationships of Google Arts &
Culture and is not using them. The partner institutions are the single most valuable
trust asset NC has; they are invisible on the product.

## The Four Questions

---

### 1. What prevents Nature & Culture from feeling like a world-class institution?

**Primary barrier: Institutional anonymity.**

A world-class institution has named humans who are publicly accountable for named
decisions. When the Smithsonian acquires a collection, a named curator is responsible.
When the Rijksmuseum releases works as CC0, a named director stated the principle
publicly. When NatGeo publishes a story about Petra, a named editor approved it.

Nature & Culture has no visible humans. There is no founder page. No director. No
named curator for any collection. No editorial staff. No research team. The governance
apparatus behind NC — which is genuinely rigorous — is invisible. A first-time visitor
cannot answer the question "who made this?" And an institution that cannot answer that
question cannot be trusted as an institution.

**Secondary barrier: The governance-to-public gap.**

NC's internal governance is more thorough than most regional museums. The authority
resolution standards, the rights matrices, the institution factory, the constitutional
invariants — these are real scholarly work, done rigorously, documented completely.
None of it is visible to the public. The work that *earns* institutional credibility
exists in private files.

The remediation is not to rebuild the governance. It is to publish it — or a
public-facing version of it — and name the people who made the decisions.

**Tertiary barrier: No permanence signal.**

Physical institutions signal permanence through stone, not policy. A digital-only
institution must signal permanence through behavior: depth of content, dated version
history on documents, archival language, stated long-term commitments. NC's content is
deep but its permanence is not signalled. Nothing on the site says "this collection
will exist in 20 years."

**Specific gaps, ranked:**

| Gap | Current state | World-class standard |
|---|---|---|
| Named staff | None visible | Curator per collection, director named |
| Mission statement | Internal docs | Publicly accessible, dated, signed |
| Selection methodology | Internal governance | Publicly accessible methodology page |
| Institutional history | Not visible | Founded, by whom, why, what changed |
| Research output | None | Published standards, cited methodology |
| Public accountability | None | Annual statement, named contact |

---

### 2. What prevents public trust?

**Primary barrier: The PD value chain is invisible.**

NC's most important public trust claim is also its most confused: "These prints are
legal to sell because the works are in the public domain." This claim is completely
correct. It is nowhere explained on the platform.

A first-time visitor who encounters a $350 print of a Wilson watercolour held by the
Natural History Museum faces a reasonable question: "How can a website sell a print
of something held by a museum?" The answer — the museum released it as CC0, the work's
copyright expired in 1912, public domain means no permission is required from anyone —
is not answered anywhere visible on NC.

The result: a visitor who doesn't understand public domain assumes NC is either (a)
a licensed business that paid the museum, which makes them wonder about the price, or
(b) a business using content without permission, which makes them distrust the whole
platform. Both readings are wrong. Both readings are invited by the current design.

**Secondary barrier: Anonymous commerce.**

Commerce requires accountability. When a visitor buys something from Rijksmuseum,
the Smithsonian, or NatGeo, they know exactly who they are buying from — a named,
chartered, physically located institution. When a visitor buys from NC, they cannot
see who runs the business, where it is located, or who to contact if something goes
wrong. Anonymous commerce is structurally indistinguishable from a dropshipping
operation. The design, however good, does not resolve this.

**Tertiary barrier: No third-party validation.**

The Smithsonian, Rijksmuseum, and NatGeo have press coverage, academic citations, awards,
and institutional endorsements that NC does not yet have. First-time visitors cannot
discover that NC's standards are high; they can only try to infer it from the design.
Inference is a poor substitute for evidence.

**Specific gaps, ranked:**

| Gap | Consequence |
|---|---|
| No public domain explainer | Visitors cannot understand why NC has the right to sell these prints |
| No visible institutional partners | The source of NC's content authority is invisible |
| No named founder/director | Anonymous commerce = structural distrust |
| No press coverage or endorsements | No third-party trust signal |
| No returns/quality policy visible | Commerce trust floor not established |
| No contact page with real details | No accountability anchor |

### 3. What prevents collector trust?

**Primary barrier: No provenance document at point of sale.**

A collector purchasing a fine art print from Rijksmuseum, Christie's, Sotheby's, or
any serious print publisher receives a certificate of authenticity that contains, at
minimum: the work title, the creator, the date of the original work, the institutional
source, the print edition number and size, the paper specification, the print process,
the print studio, and conservation guidance. This document is the difference between
a print and a collectible.

NC sells no such document. The product page contains attribution, but not in a form
a collector can keep, file, or present to an insurer or future buyer. A collector
spending $300–$500 on an archival print must be able to prove what they bought. NC
currently gives them no mechanism to do so.

**Secondary barrier: No edition architecture.**

"Limited edition" is a claim that requires a system to be true. A limited edition means:
a defined number exists (e.g., 50 prints per edition), a registry tracks which number
each buyer received, the edition closes when the number is reached, and the plates or
files are retired. Without this system, "limited edition" is marketing language, not a
collector statement.

NC has defined product lines (NC-PROD-001, NC-PROD-002) but these are governance codes,
not collector-facing edition documentation. Whether NC's editions are actually limited in
the collector sense — that is, registered and closed — is not visible to the buyer.

**Tertiary barrier: No collector relationship.**

Collectors do not just buy objects — they buy into institutions. Rijksmuseum print shop
buyers receive institutional communications. Christie's clients have specialist contacts.
A collector who has purchased from NC has no ongoing relationship, no access to future
works before general release, no acknowledgment that their purchase was meaningful.
The collector community is the highest-trust, highest-value audience NC will ever have.
NC currently does nothing to retain them after the first purchase.

**Quaternary barrier: No physical permanence signal.**

A collector commissioning a $500 archival giclée from a digital-only platform has a
reasonable concern: will this platform exist in 5 years? If the platform disappears,
the provenance of the print — "from Nature & Culture" — disappears with it. Collectors
historically require institutional permanence as part of the investment thesis. NC has
not yet signalled permanence.

**Specific gaps, ranked:**

| Gap | Collector consequence |
|---|---|
| No certificate of authenticity | Print cannot be authenticated, insured, or resold with provenance |
| No edition registry | "Limited edition" claim unverifiable |
| No print specification disclosure | Collector cannot assess archival quality or longevity |
| No conservation guidance | Collector does not know how to preserve the work |
| No institutional source link on product | Provenance chain breaks at NC — cannot be traced to the holding institution |
| No collector program | No reason for a collector to buy a second print |
| No physical/permanent presence signal | Permanence of the investment is unresolvable |

**The certificate of authenticity gap is the most urgent collector fix.** Every other
collector gap compounds it, but without this document, NC cannot make a credible claim
to the fine art print market.

### 4. What prevents educational adoption?

**Primary barrier: NC does not surface the educational affordance of public domain.**

The single most valuable thing NC has for teachers is invisible on the platform: every
illustration NC sells is freely reproducible for educational purposes. Public domain means
no licensing fee, no permission request, no copyright compliance overhead. A teacher can
print a Wilson penguin watercolour, project an Owen Jones pattern in a design class, use
a Roberts lithograph in a history lesson — all without asking NC or anyone else.

NC's platform does not say this. It says "Public Domain" in small attribution text. It
does not say "free to download and use in your classroom" anywhere visible. It does not
have a downloads page. It does not have a "for educators" section. It does not have
lesson plans, discussion guides, or curriculum alignment documentation.

The result is that the audience most likely to need free, high-quality, legally unambiguous
historical illustrations — teachers — encounters NC as a commerce platform and leaves.

**Secondary barrier: No accessibility audit or publication.**

Educational institutions procuring digital resources are required, in most jurisdictions,
to verify WCAG 2.1 AA accessibility compliance before recommending or licensing platforms
to students. NC has no published WCAG audit. No accessibility statement. No remediation
timeline. This is not a design nicety — it is a legal procurement requirement in the
US, UK, EU, and Australia. Without an accessibility statement, NC cannot be formally
adopted by any public school system.

**Tertiary barrier: No teacher resources.**

Smithsonian Education provides lesson plans, primary source sets, discussion guides,
and curriculum alignment documents for every collection. National Geographic has a
formal education program with school licensing, teacher resources, and professional
development. Google A&C has a classroom mode. NC has none of these. A teacher who
decides NC content is valuable has no infrastructure to act on that decision.

**Quaternary barrier: No educational pricing tier.**

Google A&C is free. Smithsonian online resources are free. NatGeo has a school licensing
program with educational pricing. NC sells products. A teacher who wants to use NC as a
discovery resource cannot do so for free — they encounter a commerce platform.

The distinction between "using NC's PD content for education" (free, zero friction) and
"buying a print from NC" (commerce, full friction) is not made anywhere on the platform.
The two use cases have entirely different value propositions and entirely different user
journeys. NC currently has one journey: the commerce journey.

**Specific gaps, ranked:**

| Gap | Educational consequence |
|---|---|
| No free download path for PD content | Teachers cannot use content without navigating a commerce platform |
| No WCAG accessibility audit | Platform cannot be formally adopted by any public institution |
| No teacher resources | Interested teachers have no tools to act on their interest |
| No curriculum alignment documentation | Discovery resources cannot be matched to teaching objectives |
| No educational pricing tier | Education use case has no separated pathway from commerce |
| No educational partnership signal | No institutional credibility in the education space |

## Remediation Roadmap

### Tier 0 — Pre-Gate E (must be complete before any product goes live)

These gaps are so fundamental that launching without addressing them actively harms
institutional credibility and may generate commerce complaints.

**IR-003: Certificate of authenticity**
Design and deploy a certificate for every print product. Minimum 8 fields:
- Work title and creator
- Date of original work
- Institutional source (e.g., "Collection of the Natural History Museum, London")
- Rights status and chain ("Released as CC0 by NHM, 2014. Work in public domain since 1912.")
- Edition number and size
- Paper specification (gsm, archival rating, acid-free confirmation)
- Print process and studio
- Conservation recommendation

This document ships with every physical product and is downloadable from every digital product page.

**IR-002: Public domain explainer**
A standalone page (or persistent footer element) that answers "why can NC sell this?"
in plain language. Three paragraphs:
- What public domain means
- How NC's source institutions released these works
- What "CC0" means and why it matters

This is not legal text. It is trust copy. It should be written at a reading level
accessible to any adult visitor.

**IR-004: Institutional partner acknowledgment on product**
Every product page must display, above the fold: the name of the holding institution,
a link to the source record at that institution, and the rights release statement.
"From the collection of the Natural History Museum, London. Released as CC0. View the
original record →"

This is a single design change that converts the institution's trust into NC's trust.

**IR-001: Named director / founding statement**
A single "About" page with: who founded NC, when, with what mission, and who is
responsible for collection and editorial decisions. This does not require a large staff.
It requires one named, accountable human whose name is on the institution.

---

### Tier 1 — Pre-Public Launch (before press coverage or institutional outreach)

These gaps will be encountered by the first journalists, educators, and collectors who
engage with NC and will determine whether early coverage is credible.

**IR-009: WCAG accessibility audit**
Commission a WCAG 2.1 AA audit before any formal outreach to educational institutions.
Publish the audit result and remediation plan on the site. This is table stakes for
any institutional adoption.

**IR-005: Edition registry**
Define and implement edition architecture before any "limited edition" product ships.
The implementation can be simple (a spreadsheet or database tracking edition number per
buyer per product) but the policy must be public: how many prints per edition, what
happens when an edition closes, how buyers can verify their edition number.

**IR-008: Bylines on editorial content**
Every collection page, every essay, every place description should carry a named author
or be attributed to a named editorial role ("Curated by [Name], NC Editorial"). Anonymous
editorial is not editorial.

**IR-007: Publish the governance standards**
A public-facing version of NC's authority standards — not the full internal governance
documents, but the key claims: how places are selected, how rights are verified, how
illustrations are identified as PD, what standards NC uses to assess institutional
source quality. This answers the Smithsonian curator's question.

**IR-010: Free download path for PD content**
Create an "Educator Downloads" page where teachers can download PD illustrations in
educational resolution (suitable for classroom projection and printing, not commercial
resolution) with zero friction and no commerce intent required. Include a clear license
statement: "These works are in the public domain. No permission is required to use
them for educational purposes."

**IR-011 + IR-006: Print specification disclosure**
Add a "Print specifications" section to every physical product page: paper gsm and
brand, archival rating, print process (giclée = inkjet at archival quality, this should
be stated plainly), UV-resistance rating, frame recommendation, and conservation guidance.

---

### Tier 2 — Post-Launch Institution Building (ongoing)

These gaps are real but addressable over time and do not block the initial launch.

**IR-013: Teacher resources**
Begin with the 5 signature collections. For each, produce:
- 1-page context guide for teachers (place, illustrator, historical moment)
- 3 discussion questions per collection
- 1 curriculum alignment note (history, biology, geography, art)

This is not a curriculum. It is a hook. It invites teachers into a relationship.

**IR-014: Collector program**
Define the minimum viable collector program: a mailing list that provides early access
to new editions, a collector acknowledgment in the certificate (first buyer of an
edition receives a "founding collection" note), and a stated relationship between the
collector and the institution ("NC Founding Collectors").

**IR-016: Institutional history**
Write and publish the founding narrative. When did NC begin, what was the founding
problem it was solving, how has the governance evolved, what decisions were made and
why. This is not a corporate "about" page. It is an institutional history — the kind
that the Smithsonian and Rijksmuseum have and that establishes permanence.

**IR-017: Source institution link from product**
Every product page should link to the original record at the source institution.
This is both a trust signal and a provenance tool — a collector who wants to trace
the Wilson print to its NHM source record can do so in one click.

**IR-015: Third-party validation (earned)**
Press coverage, institutional endorsements, and academic citations cannot be purchased
— they must be earned. The actions in Tier 0 and Tier 1 create the conditions for
a museum professional to recommend NC in a publication, for a journalist to write
about the PD commerce model, for an educator to cite NC in a curriculum guide. This
gap closes as a consequence of the others.

---

## Institutional Identity Summary

The audit reveals a consistent pattern: NC has done the institutional work but has not
yet done the institutional signalling. The rights are sound. The governance is rigorous.
The content selection is principled. The editorial voice is correct. None of this is
visible to a visitor, a collector, an educator, or a journalist encountering NC for the
first time.

The reference institutions — Smithsonian, Rijksmuseum, NatGeo, Google A&C — built their
trust over decades of visible decisions made by named people. NC cannot shortcut that
timeline. But it can front-load the signals: name the humans, publish the standards,
document the provenance, acknowledge the partners, and give collectors the paperwork
they need.

An institution that knows its own quality and keeps it private is not yet an institution.
It is a practice.

The gap between where NC is today and where it needs to be is not a design gap or a
content gap. It is a disclosure gap. The work exists. It must be made visible.
