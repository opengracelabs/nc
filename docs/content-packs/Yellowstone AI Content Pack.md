## 1. Story Version: "The Living Frontier"
Yellowstone is the story of a world where the Earth’s inner heat meets the raw wildness of the American West. In 1872, it became the world’s first national park—a revolutionary idea that a landscape could be so extraordinary it must belong to all people for all time. 

From the thundering weight of the American Bison (*Bison bison*) to the geothermal breath of Old Faithful, Yellowstone is a primordial forge. It is a place where 19th-century explorers like those of the Hayden Expedition (NARA) first documented the "sublime" wonders of the Grand Canyon of the Yellowstone. Today, we bridge this history with modern intelligence, seeing the park not just as a destination, but as a living record of resilience and geological power.

## 3. Educational Version: "Geothermal Ecology & Extremophiles"
**Module: Life at the Limits**

Yellowstone hosts the world’s most concentrated collection of hydrothermal features. Beyond the geysers, these "Hot Springs" are biological laboratories.

**Key Learning Points:**
*   **Extremophiles:** Explore the thermophilic microbes (GBIF) that thrive in boiling, acidic waters, providing a window into the early Earth and the search for life on other planets.
*   **Thermal Signatures:** NASA Landsat data (NC-NASA-026) allows scientists to map the park’s heat zones from orbit, tracking changes in the volcanic caldera.
*   **Ecosystem Balance:** Trace the "Yellowstone Reborn" narrative, where the reintroduction of the Gray Wolf (*Canis lupus*)—documented in NARA policy records—restored balance to the park’s biodiversity.

## 4. Student Version: "The Land of Fire and Ice"
**Field Guide: The First National Park**

Did you know that Yellowstone sits on top of a giant "supervolcano"? It’s a place where the ground can be hot enough to boil water, but the winters are cold enough to freeze everything in sight.

In the 1800s, people told stories of "fire and brimstone" in the West, but many didn't believe them. It took explorers with notebooks and cameras (like those in the BHL and NARA archives) to prove it was real. Today, you can see the same Bison that George Catlin sketched over 150 years ago. Yellowstone reminds us that when we protect a place, we save its stories and its animals forever.
