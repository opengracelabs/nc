# Universal Media Experience Strategy v1

**Role:** Chief Curator  
**Objective:** Define how users experience 11 distinct media types—from 3D models to audiobooks—within a unified, place-centered platform focused on Place, Story, and Atmosphere.

---

## 1. The Core Philosophy: The Place is the Container

In traditional archives, media is sorted by format (e.g., "The Photo Gallery," "The Map Room"). In Nature & Culture, **the Place is the container.** A user visits "Yellowstone 1872," and the media—whether a 3D fossil, an audiobook snippet, or a high-res photograph—naturally emerges to tell the story of that place.

### The "Genius Loci" (Spirit of Place) Experience
When a user enters a "Place," the platform dynamically orchestrates the media to create an atmosphere. It does not feel like scrolling through a list; it feels like standing in a curated physical space.

---

## 2. Experiencing the Media (The 11 Formats)

### Visual Art & Photography (Images, Posters, Photography)
*   **Discovery:** Never presented as isolated thumbnails. Images are introduced contextually within the landscape or narrative of the place.
*   **Atmosphere:** **The "Infinite Zoom."** (Rijksmuseum model). The user is encouraged to look closer. The UI fades away, allowing the user to become lost in the brushstrokes or silver gelatin grain.
*   **Storytelling:** Images are anchored with "Close Look" narratives—guided tours highlighting specific details (e.g., the specific type of flora in a Moran painting).

### Cartography (Maps)
*   **Discovery:** Maps are the "Ground Floor." They provide the spatial context for all other media.
*   **Atmosphere:** **The "Temporal Overlay."** Historical maps fade into modern topographies. The user experiences the passage of time physically through the changing landscape.
*   **Storytelling:** "Scrollytelling" (NatGeo model) guides users along historical expedition routes, triggering photos or journal entries as they "travel" the map.

### Textual & Spoken Narrative (Books, eBooks, Audiobooks, Audio)
*   **Discovery:** Text is never a wall; it is a companion. Snippets of journals or field notes appear alongside the visual media they describe.
*   **Atmosphere:** **The "Binaural Presence."** (BBC Earth model). Audio is spatial. Reading a field note about a geyser is accompanied by the ambient, 3D sound of that geyser erupting.
*   **Education:** "Contextual Bridges" allow users to pause an audiobook to explore a 3D model of the animal currently being described.

### Cinematic & Spatial Media (Film, 3D)
*   **Discovery:** Treated as "Spectacles." These are high-impact moments within the place journey.
*   **Atmosphere:** **"Cinematic Immersion"** (Apple TV+ model). For film, the interface goes dark; it is edge-to-edge. For 3D, the object exists in a "Void Space" tailored to its era (e.g., a fossil sits in warm, dusty museum lighting).
*   **Storytelling:** 3D objects can be virtually "held" and rotated. Annotations act as a docent, explaining the significance of the object's physical form.

### Informational Data (Datasets)
*   **Discovery:** Data is the "Undercurrent." It is not presented as a spreadsheet but as an interactive visualization layered over the place.
*   **Education:** Complex migration patterns or climate data become visual, animated layers on the map, transforming abstract numbers into tangible realities.

---

## 3. The Functional Domains

### 3.1 Place Discovery
*   **The "Flyover":** Users enter a place from a macro view, diving down through atmospheric layers (clouds, soundscapes) before landing on specific media assets.

### 3.2 Storytelling
*   **The "Discovery Flywheel" (Google Arts & Culture):** Exploring a photo of a wolf in Yellowstone seamlessly connects the user to an audiobook chapter on wolf reintroduction, which connects to a 3D model of a wolf skull.

### 3.3 Atmosphere
*   **Scenographic Minimalism:** The UI only exists to serve the media. If a user pauses on a sunset photograph, the platform's color palette and ambient audio subtly shift to match the "Golden Hour."

### 3.4 Education
*   **The "Two Doors":** Users can choose to simply "Experience" (lean back, watch/listen) or "Investigate" (lean forward, zoom, annotate, read datasets).

### 3.5 Tourism
*   **The "Regenerative Gateway":** The media builds empathy for the place. After experiencing the deep history and fragile ecosystem via the media, users are guided toward mindful, physical visitation practices.

### 3.6 Commerce
*   **The "Patron's Gallery":** Users can purchase ultra-high-fidelity prints (Posters, Photography) or fund the digitization of specific un-scanned assets (Books, 3D models) related to the place they just experienced.

---

## 4. Universal Recommendations

### Media Hierarchy
1.  **Spatial Context (Maps):** Ground the user.
2.  **Sensory Anchor (Audio/Film):** Establish the mood and scale.
3.  **Detailed Investigation (Images/3D):** Provide the deep, intimate connection.
4.  **Intellectual Scaffolding (Text/Data):** Offer the "why" behind the experience.

### Place-First Experience
Always introduce media *because* of where it is, not what it is. A photo is not just a photo; it is a "Window into Yellowstone, 1872."

### Visual Principles
*   **Edge-to-Edge Default:** Media should fill the screen. Let the asset breathe.
*   **Tactile Fidelity:** Emphasize the physical nature of the original media (the grain of paper, the patina of bronze).

### Audio Principles
*   **Always-On Ambience:** Provide an underlying spatial soundscape that matches the location and era, even when viewing static images.

### Film Principles
*   **Pacing:** Use film sparingly to punctuate the experience. Treat it as a "Main Event" rather than background noise.
