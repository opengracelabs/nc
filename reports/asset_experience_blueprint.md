# NC-EXPERIENCE-RESEARCH-001: Asset Experience Blueprint

**Document:** NC-EXPERIENCE-RESEARCH-001  
**Version:** 1.0  
**Status:** **AUTHORITATIVE RESEARCH**  
**Date:** 2026-06-14  

This blueprint defines the emotional and intellectual "experiences" visitors desire when interacting with Nature & Culture assets. Moving beyond static templates, it aligns each asset category with specific visitor intent, drawing from the reference models of National Geographic, the Smithsonian, Google Arts & Culture, and the Rijksmuseum.

---

## 1. Masterpiece Illustrations (The Aesthetic Reveal)
*Audubon, Gould, Haeckel, Redouté*

- **What visitors actually want:** To be arrested by beauty and then wonder at the technical skill required to capture it.
- **Reference Model:** **Rijksmuseum / Google Arts & Culture.** The "Art Camera" experience where resolution is the primary form of access.
- **The Experience:** **Reverence.** 
    - The image is the hero. Silent, full-bleed presentation.
    - Extreme-resolution zoom that reveals the copper-plate line, the lithographic grain, or the watercolour bleed.
    - A narrative that emphasizes the "Human Touch" — the years of labor spent on a single plate.
    - **Outcome:** The visitor feels they are in the presence of an irreplaceable work of art, not a digital file.

## 2. Scientific Diagrams (The Intellectual Epiphany)
*Humboldt’s Tableau Physique, Haeckel’s Siphonophorae, Vesalius*

- **What visitors actually want:** To see the hidden "blueprint" of the world and understand a complex system at a glance.
- **Reference Model:** **Smithsonian / National Geographic.** Clear, authoritative information design that translates complexity into insight.
- **The Experience:** **Clarity.**
    - Interactive annotation. Hovering over a section of a diagram reveals the underlying data (e.g., the altitude-specific plants on Humboldt’s Chimborazo).
    - The "Aha!" moment where the relationship between two variables (e.g., altitude and climate) becomes visible.
    - Framing the asset as "The Origin of an Idea."
    - **Outcome:** The visitor feels they have gained a new "lens" through which to see the natural world.

## 3. Exploration Records (The Adventure by Proxy)
*Cook/Banks Florilegium, Catherwood’s Ruins, Wilson’s Antarctica*

- **What visitors actually want:** To feel the heat of the jungle, the cold of the ice, and the shock of first discovery.
- **Reference Model:** **National Geographic.** "On Assignment" storytelling. The asset as a witness to a specific, high-stakes moment.
- **The Experience:** **Discovery.**
    - Contextualized in a "Voyage Timeline." Where was the artist on the day this was drawn? What was the weather? What was the risk?
    - Juxtaposition of the 19th-century sketch with a modern 360° or satellite view of the exact same coordinate.
    - Focus on the "Evidence" — the coffee stain on the page, the hurried field note.
    - **Outcome:** The visitor feels they have joined the expedition, stepping into the explorer’s boots.

## 4. Extinction Records (The Last Witness)
*Thylacine, Passenger Pigeon, Ivory-billed Woodpecker*

- **What visitors actually want:** An emotional connection to what is lost and a sense of stewardship for what remains.
- **Reference Model:** **Smithsonian / National Geographic.** The "Natural History Memorial." Dignified, poignant, and authoritative.
- **The Experience:** **Remembrance.**
    - A "Silence" in the design. Muted palettes, focus on the individual species.
    - The "Countdown to Zero." Narrative marking the years between the illustration and the final confirmed sighting.
    - The "Ecological Ghost" — visualizing the gap in the ecosystem left by this species' departure.
    - **Outcome:** The visitor feels a profound sense of loss that translates into a desire to protect extant "Living Archive" species.

## 5. Cultural Masterworks (The Deep Context)
*Hokusai’s Great Wave, Petra Treasury, Alhambra Geometry*

- **What visitors actually want:** To touch the "Soul" of a civilization and understand the universal patterns of human creativity.
- **Reference Model:** **Rijksmuseum / Google Arts & Culture.** Cultural icons presented with deep contextual layers.
- **The Experience:** **Resonance.**
    - Connecting the geometry of the architecture (Alhambra) to the mathematics of nature (Haeckel).
    - Translating "Craft" into "Context." How was the Hokusai woodblock actually carved? What did the Petra Treasury signify to a Nabataean trader?
    - Highlighting the "Cross-Pollination" of styles across the Silk Road or Atlantic.
    - **Outcome:** The visitor feels part of a global, multi-generational human story.

## 6. Place Records (The Grounding of Truth)
*Hayden/Powell Survey Maps, NARA Cartography, Pilot Places*

- **What visitors actually want:** To locate themselves in the story and verify that the "Art" is grounded in "Fact."
- **Reference Model:** **National Geographic.** Cartography as the ultimate authority. The map as the source of truth.
- **The Experience:** **Grounding.**
    - The map as a "Gateway." Clicking a coordinate on a 19th-century survey map opens the modern reality of that place.
    - "Layered Reality." Sliding between the historical survey, the satellite image, and the artistic landscape painting.
    - Focus on "Precision." Highlighting the survey instruments and the mathematical effort required to "claim" the place for the record.
    - **Outcome:** The visitor trusts the entire platform because the geography is demonstrably real.

## 7. Taxonomic Records (The Ordering of Life)
*Linnaean Illustrations, EIC Bengal Surveys, Specialist Monographs*

- **What visitors actually want:** To see the "Infinite Variety" of life organized into a coherent and beautiful system.
- **Reference Model:** **Smithsonian.** The "Cabinet of Curiosities" modernized. Rigorous, exhaustive, yet visually spectacular.
- **The Experience:** **Variation.**
    - Grid-based exploration. Seeing 50 variations of a single genus (e.g., Redouté’s Roses) side-by-side to appreciate minor mutations.
    - The "Naming Moment." Connecting the illustration to the first scientific description.
    - Focus on "Structure." Anatomical cutaways and skeletal overlays.
    - **Outcome:** The visitor feels the awe of biodiversity through the lens of scientific order.

## 8. Planetary Records (The Overview Effect)
*Earthrise, NASA Apollo Archive, Satellite Vistas*

- **What visitors actually want:** A shift in perspective — a reminder of the Earth’s fragility and our shared place upon it.
- **Reference Model:** **NASA / National Geographic.** The "Overview Effect." Existential scale combined with intimate detail.
- **The Experience:** **Perspective.**
    - The "Infinite Zoom." Starting from the Earthrise view of the planet and zooming in to a specific pilot place (e.g., Yellowstone).
    - Minimalist, space-black UI. No distractions. The planet as the only light source.
    - Narrative focus on "Fragility." The thin blue line of the atmosphere, the isolation of the "Oasis."
    - **Outcome:** The visitor leaves with a renewed sense of global responsibility and a wider view of history.
